General Waveguide Bend Design Based on Cubic
Spline Interpolation and Inverse Design
Enge Zhang , Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Shanglin Yang , and Lei Zhang , Member, IEEE
Abstract—Silicon waveguide bends are key components that
affect the packing density and scalability of photonic integrated
circuits. Traditional bend design methods rely on well-established
functions such as Euler spirals or Bezier curves, which have lim-
ited parameter spaces, limiting the discovery of compact bends
with higher performance. Here, we propose a general approach to
waveguide bend design. For a given radius, our method uses cubic
spline interpolation with an inverse design algorithm to obtain
an optimal bend geometry to support a given number of modes.
We reﬁne the particle swarm optimization algorithm to reduce
the need for parameter tuning and increase its ability to avoid
local optima. As a result of these modiﬁcations, the design process
becomes less complicated and more efﬁcient. To demonstrate the
effectiveness of the proposed method, we design and fabricate three
waveguide bends supporting one, two, and three modes with radii
of 2.5 µm, 4 µm, and 5 µm, respectively. The measured (simulated)
insertion losses of the three bends are less than 0.012 (0.009) dB, 0.04
(0.028) dB and 0.059 (0.048) dB per 9 ◦, respectively. The measured
(simulated) crosstalk between the modes of the latter two bends
is less than −23.6 ( −36.1) dB and −19.7 ( −26.5) dB per 9 ◦,
respectively. The proposed method provides an effective tool for
designing compact multimode bends for future photonic integrated
circuits.
Index Terms—Compact bend, cubic spline interpolation, inverse
design.

Recently, the inverse design method has become a widely
used approach for developing high-performance nanophotonic
devices. The inverse design method uses automated design
algorithms [38], [39], [40] to alleviate the challenges of manual
design, thus freeing designers from tedious work. The inverse
design methodology has also been used to design waveguide
bends [41], [42]. At present, inverse design algorithms mainly
consist of heuristic algorithms, gradient algorithms, neural net-
work algorithms, etc.[11], [43], [44], [45], [46], [47]. The latter
two methods are typically complex and require a considerable
amount of time and effort to master. In practical applications,
both methods require signiﬁcant parameter tuning, and the latter
method also requires the acquisition of a large amount of data. In
contrast, heuristic algorithms are characterized by their elegant
simplicity and easy mastery. Particle swarm optimization (PSO)
is a heuristic algorithm that has found many applications in
various ﬁelds due to its simplicity and ease of use[48]. However,
the PSO algorithm is prone to get trapped in local optima, has low
search precision, and requires considerable tuning in practical
design.
To overcome the above problems, we reﬁne the traditional
PSO algorithm to balance its local and global search capabilities.
After modiﬁcation, it has the ability to escape local optima and
converge to the global optimum even in cases where suboptimal
parameters are used (at the cost of additional iterations). We refer
to our improved algorithm as the self-adaptive particle swarm
optimization (SAPSO) algorithm.
We have combined the cubic spline interpolation method
with the SAPSO algorithm, which allows the design of low-loss
bends of arbitrary width, radius and given mode. To verify the
effectiveness of the method, we designed and experimentally
veriﬁed single, single mode, dual mode and triple mode silicon
waveguide bends with widths of 500 nm, 900 nm and 1400 nm,
respectively. The effective radii of the three bends are 2.5μm,
4 μm, and 5μm, respectively.

Choosing the appropriate number of MPs is a critical issue.
Fig. 5 shows some examples of the ﬁtted curves with different
numbers of MPs. It can be seen that as the number of MPs
increases, the geometric structure of the curve becomes more
complex. Potentially, a complex curve with a larger parameter
space may result in geometries that have superior performance,
but also result in a larger number of less efﬁcient structures, thus
complicating our search process. In addition, the ﬁne structures
on the waveguide sidewall can be a manufacturing challenge.
Therefore, in the actual design, we choose to start with a small
number of MPs for the waveguide bend design, for example,
one MP on each side of the inner and outer curves of the bend,
and then increase the number of MPs as needed to meet our
insertion loss and number of supported modes requirements.

C. Algorithm Analysis
Particle swarm optimization (PSO) was proposed by Eberhart
and Kennedy in 1995[48]. PSO simulates the foraging behavior
of birds. It maps the search space for a speciﬁc problem to the
ﬂight space of a ﬂock of birds. Each bird is regarded as a particle
without mass and volume, which represents a possible solution
to the problem. The process of ﬁnding the optimal solution of
the optimization problem is just like the process of birds looking
for food, which is the result of a candidate solution. PSO regards
individuals in the population as particles in the N-dimensional
search space. Each particle moves in the space at a certain
speed and realizes the evolution of candidate solutions under the
guidance of its own experience and the experience of the group.
In our design, each particle represents a speciﬁc type of curved
geometry. The position of each particle indicates the value of
MP, while the velocity reﬂects the rate at which MP changes.
The goal of the particle swarm optimization is to identify the
curve geometry that yields the minimum insertion loss in the
current conﬁguration.

In the iterative process, it evaluates whether the current solution

solution. In each iteration, the optimal value and corresponding
position found in the history of a single particle will be recorded
as the individual experience of the particle. The optimal values
and corresponding positions found in the history of all particles
will also be recorded as the experience of the population. The
direction of motion of each particle will be determined by indi-
vidual experience and population experience. Among them, the
proportion of individual experience and population experience in
each movement speed will change randomly due to the existence
of random numbersr1 and r2, thus bringing certain randomness
to the algorithm.
In order to ensure a comprehensive search of the parameter
space, the algorithm must possess global search capabilities to
locate the range where optimal values reside, which necessitates
greater speed. However, once the optimal value range has been
determined, the algorithm must employ local search abilities
to pinpoint its exact location, requiring slightly less speed.
Regarding the particle search process, the algorithm’s early stage
should exhibit strong global search capabilities but weak local
search abilities. Conversely, in the algorithm’s later stages, its
local search abilities are robust, while its global search capabil-
ities weaken. However, in traditional PSO, the global and local
search abilities are ﬁxed once the algorithm parameters are set.
Therefore, achieving good FOM requires extensive parameter
tuning.

D. Design Methodology
The design of curve shape using cubic spline interpolation
requires two prerequisites: explicit boundary conditions and
MPs. The analysis in SectionII-B indicates that the clamped
boundary condition is employed for curves located on the inner
side of the bend, while the not-a-knot boundary condition is
utilized for curves situated on the outer side of the bend. If it
is determined that the design requirements cannot be fulﬁlled,
adjustments will be made in accordance with the value size
relationship depicted in Fig.5. Based on our design expertise,
it is generally unnecessary to adjust the boundary conditions.
According to the analysis in SectionII-C, the known points
consist of MPs and endpoints, with the algorithm optimizing
the coordinates of MPs. To simplify algorithm design, polar
coordinates are adopted for representing MP coordinates while
only modifying their radius coordinates by ﬁxing their angle
coordinates. Initially, a small number of MPs is selected and in-
creased if necessary to meet design requirements. To summarize,
the design process for a waveguide bend is as follows:
1) Determine the width, radius, and number of modes of the
bend to be designed.
2) Set an initial number of MPs, such as one MP each on
the inside and outside of the bend, and set the clamped
and not-a-knot boundary conditions inside and outside the
bend respectively.
3) Establish the model structure and the MPs’ coordinates
automatically through SAPSO.
4) If the design outcome fails to meet the requirements,
increase the number of MPs and repeat step (3). If the
requirements are still not satisﬁed, adjust the boundary
conditions as indicated in the aforementioned analysis.
It is worth noting that the deﬁnition of FOM employs a
simplistic approach whereby the average insertion loss of all
target modes is calculated.

IV . CONCLUSION
In conclusion, we propose an efﬁcient approach to waveguide
curve design that uses cubic spline interpolation and an improved
particle swarm optimization algorithm to determine the curve
geometry. The proposed method not only provides a larger
parameter space compared to traditional design methods, but
also exhibits remarkable ﬂexibility in designing waveguides
with arbitrary widths, radii, and mode numbers (supported by the
waveguide) according to the speciﬁc requirements. Meanwhile,
the SAPSO algorithm based on the traditional PSO algorithm
proposed in this paper avoids the problem of falling into local
extremums, and can still ﬁnd the global optimal value when the
algorithm parameters are not appropriate (at the cost of requiring
more iterations). This feature makes the design process very
simple compared to other inverse design methods. At the same
time, due to the simplicity of the structure, the algorithm can also
reach the optimal result quickly. Using the design method, we
have successfully designed single-mode, dual-mode, and triple-
mode waveguide bends with radii of 2.5μm, 4μm, and 5μm,
respectively. The device was fabricated by e-beam lithography,
which gives excellent experimental results and is in agreement
with simulations.
The proposed method is simple, efﬁcient, and versatile in ac-
commodating different widths, radii, and modes for waveguide
bending. Its potential for large-scale photon integration makes
it a signiﬁcant contribution to the advancement of this ﬁeld.
